Tax Filing Essentials

Understanding Your Filing Requirements

Most U.S. citizens and permanent residents must file a tax return if their income exceeds certain thresholds. For 2023, single filers under 65 must file if they earned $12,950 or more, while married couples filing jointly have a $25,900 threshold. Special rules apply for self-employed individuals earning $400 or more.

Key Documents You'll Need

  • W-2 forms from all employers
  • 1099 forms for freelance/contract work
  • Investment income statements (1099-INT, 1099-DIV)
  • Mortgage interest statements (Form 1098)
  • Receipts for deductible expenses

Maximizing Your Deductions

Standard vs. Itemized Deductions

For 2023, the standard deduction is $13,850 for single filers and $27,700 for married couples filing jointly. Itemizing makes sense if your eligible expenses (mortgage interest, state taxes, charitable contributions) exceed these amounts.

Top Overlooked Deductions

  • Student loan interest (up to $2,500)
  • Educator expenses (up to $300)
  • Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI
  • State sales tax (if you live in a no-income-tax state)
  • Home office expenses for self-employed

Choosing Your Filing Method

Method Cost Best For
Paper Filing Free Simple returns
IRS Free File Free (income <$73k) Basic returns
Tax Software $0-$120+ Most filers
CPA $200-$1000+ Complex situations

E-filing typically processes refunds in 21 days vs 6-8 weeks for paper filing. Most states offer free e-file options when you file federal taxes.

Avoiding Filing Mistakes

Top Audit Triggers

  • Math errors or transposed numbers
  • Unreported income (all 1099s and W-2s must be included)
  • Overstating charitable deductions
  • Claiming ineligible dependents
  • Home office deductions for employees (not self-employed)

Document Retention

Keep all tax records for at least 3 years from filing date (7 years if claiming loss from worthless securities). Digital scans are acceptable if legible. Organize by year and category (income, deductions, investments).
