Tax Filing Fundamentals

Understanding Your Filing Requirements

Most U.S. citizens and permanent residents must file a tax return if their income exceeds certain thresholds. For 2023, single filers under 65 must file if they earned $12,950 or more, while married couples filing jointly have a $25,900 threshold.

Choosing the Right Filing Status

Your filing status significantly impacts your tax rate and deductions. The five statuses are: Single, Married Filing Jointly, Married Filing Separately, Head of Household, and Qualifying Widow(er) with Dependent Child. Selecting the optimal status can save you thousands.

Essential Documents Checklist

  • W-2 forms from all employers
  • 1099 forms for freelance/contract work
  • Investment income statements (1099-INT, 1099-DIV)
  • Mortgage interest statements (Form 1098)
  • Student loan interest statements
  • Receipts for deductible expenses
  • Last year's tax return for reference

Maximizing Deductions & Credits

Standard vs. Itemized Deductions

For 2023, the standard deduction is $13,850 for single filers and $27,700 for married couples filing jointly. Itemizing makes sense if your eligible expenses (mortgage interest, state taxes, charitable contributions, medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI) exceed these amounts.

Top Tax Credits to Claim

Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

For low-to-moderate income workers, worth up to $7,430 for families with 3+ children.

Child Tax Credit

Up to $2,000 per qualifying child, with $1,600 potentially refundable.

Education Credits

American Opportunity Credit (up to $2,500) and Lifetime Learning Credit (up to $2,000).

Overlooked Deductions

Many taxpayers miss deductions for: educator expenses, student loan interest paid by parents, HSA contributions, home office expenses for self-employed, and state sales tax in lieu of state income tax.

Avoiding Filing Mistakes

Top Errors That Trigger Audits

Math errors or transposed numbers

Unreported income (all 1099s and W-2s must be included)

Overstating charitable deductions without documentation

Claiming ineligible dependents

Documentation Best Practices

Keep all tax records for at least 3 years from filing date (7 years if claiming loss from worthless securities or bad debt). Digital scans are acceptable if legible. Organize by:

  • Income documents (W-2s, 1099s)
  • Deduction receipts
  • Investment purchase records
  • Home improvement receipts
  • Medical expense records

What to Do If You Make a Mistake

File an amended return using Form 1040-X if you discover errors after filing. For math errors, the IRS will typically correct these automatically. For missing forms or significant omissions, amend within 3 years of original filing to claim refunds.
